In the constant search for higher conversion rates and sales volume, the most widely used tool by e-commerce managers is the direct discount. Welcome coupons of 10%, seasonal markdowns of 20%, or flash promotions of 30% are applied casually under the premise that "more sales compensate for the price reduction."

However, from a strictly financial perspective, aggressive discounts act like acid on the contribution margin. By directly cutting the final selling price without reducing the variable costs of product acquisition, logistics, or payment gateways, discounts force marketing campaigns to operate at extraordinarily high performance levels just to avoid losses. In this analysis, we will break down the mathematics of the impact of discounts on your net advertising margin and calculate the exact volume increase required for a promotion to be profitable.

---

## The Mathematics of Discounting: Impact on the Contribution Margin

To assess the impact of a discount, we must first define the cost structure of a standard product. The original selling price ($P$) is made up of variable costs ($C$, which encompass COGS, shipping, payment commissions, and storage) and the gross margin in absolute value ($M$):

$$P = C + M$$

The initial gross margin percentage ($M_\%$) is expressed as:

$$M_\% = \frac{P - C}{P} = \frac{M}{P}$$

When we apply a discount rate ($d$, expressed in decimal format), the new selling price is $P' = P(1 - d)$. Since variable costs ($C$) remain unchanged (suppliers don't charge us less and transport companies don't lower their rates because of the discount), the new absolute gross margin ($M'$) becomes:

$$M' = P(1 - d) - C = (P - C) - d \cdot P = M - d \cdot P$$

And the new gross margin percentage ($M'_\%$) is reduced to:

$$M'_\% = \frac{P(1 - d) - C}{P(1 - d)}$$

### Practical Example:
Imagine an online store that sells backpacks:
* **Original price ($P$):** €100
* **Unit variable costs ($C$):** €40 (COGS: €25, Shipping: €12, Payment gateway: €3)
* **Original gross margin ($M$):** $100 - 40 = 60\ \text{€}\ (60\%)$

If this store applies a **20%** discount ($d = 0.20$):
* **New price ($P'$):** €80
* **Variable costs ($C$):** €40 (remain identical)
* **New gross margin ($M'$):** $80 - 40 = 40\ \text{€}\ (50\%)$

Although the selling price has only dropped 20%, the gross margin in absolute value has fallen from €60 to €40. This represents a **33.3% reduction in your unit gross profit**. You have sacrificed a third of your earnings on every unit sold.

## How Much Additional Volume Do You Need to Compensate for the Discount?

To maintain the same total gross profit before marketing expenses, unit sales must increase exponentially. The formula to determine the required volume increase ($I_{\text{volume}}$) is:

$$I_{\text{volume}} = \frac{d}{M_\% - d} \times 100$$

Where $d$ is the discount rate and $M_\%$ is the original gross margin expressed in decimals.

Following the previous example of a 60% margin ($0.60$) and applying a 20% discount ($0.20$):

$$I_{\text{volume}} = \frac{0.20}{0.60 - 0.20} \times 100 = \frac{0.20}{0.40} \times 100 = 50\%$$

This means that by applying a 20% discount, **you must sell 50% more physical units** simply to break even with the gross profit you had before the discount. If you previously sold 100 backpacks and earned €6,000 gross, you now need to sell 150 backpacks to get the same €6,000.

What happens if the initial gross margin is lower, for example 30% ($0.30$), and you apply the same 20% discount?

$$I_{\text{volume}} = \frac{0.20}{0.30 - 0.20} \times 100 = \frac{0.20}{0.10} \times 100 = 200\%$$

For a product with a 30% margin, offering a 20% discount forces you to **triple your sales** (a 200% increase) to achieve the same absolute profit. This is the most direct path to financial insolvency for an e-commerce business.

## The Critical Impact on the Breakeven ROAS

Paid advertising investment adds another layer of complexity. To assess the viability of an advertising campaign under a discount strategy, we must look at the impact on the **Breakeven ROAS** (break-even point ROAS).

Recall the Breakeven ROAS formula:

$$\text{Breakeven ROAS} = \frac{1}{\text{Gross Margin (\%)}}$$

Let's analyze how this threshold changes for the backpack in the previous example:

* **Without discount (60% margin / 0.60):**
  $$\text{Breakeven ROAS} = \frac{1}{0.60} = 1.67$$

* **With 20% discount (50% margin / 0.50):**
  $$\text{Breakeven ROAS} = \frac{1}{0.50} = 2.00$$

By applying the discount, the minimum ROAS required to avoid losing money goes from 1.67 to 2.00.

If the advertising campaign was operating at a ROAS of 1.90:
* **Without discount:** It generated net profits (ROAS 1.90 > 1.67).
* **With a 20% discount:** It starts generating operating losses (ROAS 1.90 < 2.00), unless the conversion rate rises sharply to compensate for the revenue drop and push the real ROAS above 2.00.

## Financial Case Study: Advertising Campaign Simulation

Let's put two real acquisition scenarios side by side with a fixed marketing budget of €10,000 and a cost per click (CPC) of €0.50 (20,000 clicks).

| Metric | Scenario A: Full Price | Scenario B: 20% Discount |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Selling Price (AOV)** | €100 | €80 |
| **Unit variable cost (C)**| €40 | €40 |
| **Conversion rate (CR)** | 2.0% | 2.7% (35% increase due to offer appeal) |
| **Total orders** | 400 | 540 |
| **Gross revenue** | €40,000 | €43,200 |
| **ROAS achieved** | 4.00 | 4.32 |
| **Unit Gross Margin** | €60 | €40 |
| **Total Gross Margin** | €24,000 | €21,600 |
| **Ad Spend** | €10,000 | €10,000 |
| **Net Margin (Real Profit)**| **€14,000** | **€11,600** |

### Case Analysis:
In Scenario B, the price reduction attracted more buyers, raising the conversion rate from 2.0% to 2.7% and increasing revenue by €3,200 (with a ROAS of 4.32 vs. 4.00). At first glance, the marketing manager might label it an absolute success.

However, looking at the bottom line, the actual net profit has dropped from €14,000 to €11,600 (a drop of €2,400). You had to package, ship, and provide support to **140 additional customers** to end up earning **less net money**. Additionally, operational wear and the risk of returns have multiplied at no extra cost.

## Strategic Alternatives to Direct Discounts

If direct discounts erode your net margin so severely, how can we stimulate sales and increase conversion rates during slow periods? The answer lies in **adding perceived value** rather than reducing the price.

1. **The structured BOGO (Buy One Get One) model:**
   Instead of a direct 50% discount on one product, offer a "Buy one, get the second at half price" deal (equivalent to an average 25% discount). The big difference is that you force the customer to acquire two units, spreading the logistical shipping cost across a single delivery and increasing the volume of product moved.

2. **Gift with Purchase:**
   Offer a low-production-cost complementary product (low COGS but high perceived value—for example, a carrying case that costs €2 to make but is valued at €15 on the market) if the customer exceeds a minimum purchase amount. The impact on your net margin is only €2, while the conversion incentive is psychologically equivalent to a large discount.

3. **Extended warranties and free additional services:**
   Add intangible value like "Priority VIP Support for 1 Year" or "Extended 2-Year Breakage Warranty." These services have a near-zero delivery cost for the e-commerce but raise the appeal of the offer for the discerning buyer.

## Conclusion

A discount is a short-term painkiller that creates a dangerous addiction both for your customer base (who get used to never paying full price) and for the financial health of your business. Before launching any coupon campaign, it is vital to sit down and calculate the resulting margins. Using the mathematics presented here will allow you to shield your real profit from vanity metrics and ensure that every euro invested in advertising translates into sustainable net profitability.
